Simulating Actin Compression

Comparing simulators of actin filament bending and twisting reveals tradeoff between computational cost and capturing detailed behaviors.

Simularium visualization of filaments simulated in ReaDDy and Cytosim.

The dynamic bending and twisting of actin filaments mechanically drive many processes in cells.

Fundamental cellular processes such as endocytosis, cell motility, and cytokinesis are reliant on a cell's ability to produce force. The actin cytoskeleton plays a central role in force production in these processes.


We developed two models of actin to compare dynamics at different spatiotemporal scales.

There have been many independent efforts to model these properties using different spatial simulation methods that emphasize different spatiotemporal scales. However, each simulation method has limitations due to its particular implementation and assumptions characteristic of the simulated length scale.


We then simulated the compression of these filaments to 350 nm across a range of velocities.

We sought to determine the regimes in which each simulator captured the 3D mechanics of compressing actin filaments. To do this, we simulated the compression of an actin filament at five compression velocities, spanning the range of velocities that were computationally feasible and physiologically relevant.

Comparison matrix for simulated trajectories.

Our quantitative comparisons and principal component analysis revealed a divergence between the monomer-scale and fiber-scale simulations, particularly in terms of non-coplanarity.

These simulations showed systematic differences in the shapes of these bending and twisting filaments. The filaments simulated using ReaDDy had notable directional biphasic out-of-plane behavior, indicative of filament twist only captured with monomer-scale resolution. This out-of-plane helical behavior was also more evident in filaments simulated at higher compression velocities. The Cytosim simulations showed very little out-of-plane behavior at any compression velocities, and when they did, it was just as likely to be in either direction.

Notably, the monomer-scale simulations captured an out-of-plane filament twist, characteristic of a helical structure, more effectively than the fiber-scale simulations.

Inverse PCA transforms of the filament shapes confirmed that PC1 captured filament compression and peak asymmetry, while PC2 scaled with filament non-coplanarity and twist. Inverse transforms also revealed that low values of PC2 correspond to twist in the opposite direction. Some Cytosim simulations show this behavior, likely because directional helicity is not enforced in Cytosim. Overall, we conclude that compressing filaments simulated at monomer resolution capture directional out-of-plane helicity more than filaments simulated at the fiber scale.


This comparison, enhanced by broadly shareable interactive 3D visualizations, enables researchers to make informed choices when designing multi-scale models of biological systems.
